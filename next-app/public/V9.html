Prelude

A programme, not a performance.

For too long, our children with ADHD have been labeled "lazy," "problematic," and "difficult." It’s time to rewrite the parenting playbook.

Join our ADHD parenting revolution and discover how to unlock your child’s full potential. For our brand-new & improved ADHD Parenting Summit 2026, we’re bringing together 40+ leading ADHD experts over five full evenings — not three — so there is space to think, re-read your notes, and apply something before the next session begins.

What you get is a scored programme: timed to the minute, live 7:00 – 9:30 PM Eastern, replays available within 48 hours. Covering focus, impulsivity, meltdowns, screens, sleep, schoolwork, and the conversation to have with your prescriber. Designed for parents of children ages 5–17, whether diagnosed, suspected, or somewhere in between.

You will leave with one sentence you weren’t saying, one three-minute morning that holds, and one clinician’s name for the conversation you’ve been postponing.

Why this is more important than ever

Six figures, footnoted.

The earlier you learn how to support your child, the more the long-tail narrows.
  1. #1 child

    ADHD is the top mental or developmental condition among children.[1]

  2. 1 in 3 adults

    Every 3rd child with ADHD has symptoms that last into adulthood.[2]

  3. 1 in 4 households

    A child with ADHD has a 1-in-4 chance of having a parent who also has it.[3]

  4. 2–3× risk

    Children with ADHD are 2–3× more likely to develop substance abuse or addiction.[4]

  5. Higher anxiety

    More likely to develop low self-esteem, anxiety, and depression.[5]

  6. Lower grades

    Compared to peers, children with ADHD earn significantly lower school grades.[6]

But here’s the good news. The earlier you learn how to support your child, the better your chances of managing their condition and minimizing long-term issues.
